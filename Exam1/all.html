Answer 1.a¶

The Lagrangian is: $$L(\omega,\lambda,\gamma)=\frac{1}{2}\omega'\sum\omega + \lambda(m-\omega'\mu)+\gamma(1-\omega'\mathbf{1})$$

First Order Conditions: $$ \begin{aligned} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \omega}(\omega,\lambda,\gamma) &= \omega'\sum - \lambda\mu' - \gamma\mathbf{1}' = 0 \\ \\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \lambda}(\omega,\lambda,\gamma) &= m - \omega'\mu = 0 \\ \\ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \gamma}(\omega,\lambda,\gamma) &= 1 - \omega'\mathbf{1} = 0 \end{aligned} $$

Answer 2.d¶

Mathematical Explanation:¶

Since $\mu_i = 0$, we only need to compare the absolute values of $\lvert z_\alpha \rvert$ and $\left|\frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1-c}\right|$.

Since

$$\lvert z_\alpha \rvert=2.326$$

$$\left|\frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1-c}\right|=\left|\frac{0.02667}{0.01}\right|=2.667$$

Thus

$$\left|\frac{\phi(z_\alpha)}{1-c}\right| > \lvert z_\alpha \rvert$$

Therefore, $\lvert \frac{\partial ES}{\partial \omega_i}\rvert > \lvert \frac{\partial VaR}{\partial \omega_i}\rvert$

Economic Interpretation:¶

VaR only considers the quantile level, while ES considers the average loss in the tail and is therefore more sensitive to tail risk.

Therefore, changes in marginal weights have a larger impact on ES.

Compute VaR¶

The first step is to compute the daily log return, followed by the rolling standard deviation $\sigma$ based on 21 daily returns.

$$10D\;VaR_t=Factor*\sigma_t*\sqrt{10}$$

This is obtained from the daily log-return series.

Once both series have been constructed, breaches are identified on the dates for which the forward 10D return is below the 10D VaR.

Use the overall variance as the first observation of variance, and then compute the remaining values recursively.

$$\sigma_{t+1|t}^2 = \lambda\sigma_{t|t-1}^2 + (1-\lambda)r_t^2$$

In [34]:

data.loc[data.index[0], 'variance'] = overall_variance

for i in range(1, len(data)):
    variance = param * data.loc[data.index[i - 1], 'variance'] + (1 - param) * data.loc[data.index[i - 1], 'LogReturn']**2
    data.loc[data.index[i], 'variance'] = variance

Answer 4.c¶

A comparison of rolling-window volatility, EWMA, and historical simulation is given below:

EWMA exhibits the best adaptivity because it assigns exponentially higher weight to recent returns and updates volatility instantaneously.

Rolling-window volatility has moderate adaptivity due to equal weighting and slow information decay.

Historical simulation shows the weakest adaptivity because it does not model time-varying volatility and relies on a static past distribution.
